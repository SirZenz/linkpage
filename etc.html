Symbols, Themes, and Settings in Pig Hunting

Symbols

Pigs: Represent the struggle between nature and domesticity, as well as the primal instincts within the characters. The narrator reflects, "Still, there’s something obscene about pig hunting – chasing boars through the bush with snarling muscular dogs."

Glow-worms: Symbolize hidden beauty and the duality of light and darkness in life. Upon discovering them, the narrator notes, "An entire world hidden under our noses."

Food: Reflects nurturing, survival, and the connection to the land and family. The narrator describes cooking, stating, "I’d cooked a huge stew; beans and tomato, kale and carrots... Autumn surprise, I called it."

The Rifle: Represents power, control, desperation, and the act of taking life. When she decides to hunt, she admits, "I hoisted the rifle over my shoulder, released the safety and shot."

Wild Pigs: Symbolize both a threat to the family’s livelihood and the narrator's inner turmoil and conflict. She thinks, "I thought about the pig all day –... the image of blood blooming in the slit."

The Guava Tree: Represents home, connection to the land, and the disruption caused by outside threats. The narrator observes, "Loose maroon bruises of empty skins were scattered at the base of the guava."

Bread: Symbolizes nourishment, care, routine, stability, connection to the land, and transformation. She recalls, "after an hour in the oven, brown and high, folding over the edge of the tin."

Themes

Absence and Loss: The impact of Paul’s disappearance on the family is profound. The narrator reflects, "I didn’t want to tell them until I was sure he wasn’t coming back." This highlights her struggle with uncertainty.

Nature vs. Nurture: The balance between living close to the land and the responsibilities of family life is evident when she states, "We’re not farmers, but we live close to the land."

Gender Roles: The expectations placed on characters based on gender are revealed through the hunting dynamics: "He would, too. Paul knows how to fix things." This illustrates traditional roles in the family.

Family Dynamics: The impact of a parent's absence is explored when Bo asks, "Where’s Dad?" This moment underscores the children's confusion and longing.

Isolation and Loneliness: The narrator's feelings of isolation are captured in her thoughts: "It gets dark early, without the sodium glow of the city."

Nature and Hunting: The ethics of hunting are questioned as she reflects on the violence: "It’s tough, pig-skin. It takes more force than you think."

Betrayal and Trust: The narrator’s anxiety about her husband’s fidelity is palpable when she thinks about his absence, "Like Cook, like Heke, like we all are in the end, I suppose."

Identity and Belonging: The family's adjustment to rural life is evident when she recalls their move: "When we moved here, after the first lockdown, we couldn’t believe our luck."

Settings

Rural Landscape: The setting emphasizes isolation, as the narrator notes, "Driving up the metal road, forestry blocks either side tunnelling away the view."

Family Home: Represents safety, as she reflects on their new space: "The kids settled in fine, is what I am saying."

Orchards and Creeks: Symbolize the beauty of the natural world, with the narrator observing, "The air was cooler in the spruce-lined orchard."

Rural Property: A remote farm that emphasizes themes of isolation, described as "the denuded hillside, recently harvested slash down its face like an acned teenager."

Evening and Night: The darkness creates tension, as the narrator states, "When you turn on the light, the glass turns to mirror and all you can see is your own worried face."

Inside the House: Represents domestic life, capturing the routine: "I reheated Garden Surprise, cutting off fat hunks of bread and spreading them with bright yellow butter."
